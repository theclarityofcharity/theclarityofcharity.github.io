The Problem

The Abuse Stopped.
The Damage Didn't.

Childhood sexual abuse doesn't just steal childhoods. It rewires the brain, fractures identity, and quietly fuels addiction, depression, broken relationships, and suicide for decades after. The abuser is long gone. The wound remains — invisible to most, undeniable to those who carry it.

Most survivors are not in dark corners or crisis centers. They are your neighbors, your coworkers, your friends — functioning on the outside, shattered on the inside. They became experts at surviving. Nobody taught them how to heal.

The mental health system was not built for them. Standard therapy can retraumatize. Medication masks. Support groups stall. Decades pass. And the body, as neuroscientist Bessel van der Kolk documented, keeps the score.

This is not a rare tragedy. This is a public health epidemic hiding in plain sight — and the near-total absence of multidimensional care for survivors is one of the great moral failures of our time.

93%

of juvenile sexual abuse victims know their perpetrator. This is not a crime committed by strangers in shadows. It is committed at kitchen tables, in bedrooms, by the people children are supposed to trust most.

Source: Bureau of Justice Statistics

Grounded in Research

The Science Backs the Work.

Trauma is not stored primarily as a narrative memory. It is stored in the body, in the nervous system — and that is where healing must begin.
Synthesized from research by Bessel van der Kolk, M.D., Peter Levine, Ph.D., and Judith Herman, M.D.
01

The CDC classifies childhood sexual abuse as an Adverse Childhood Experience (ACE) directly linked to higher rates of heart disease, cancer, depression, and substance abuse in adulthood. This is a physical health crisis as much as a mental one.

02

Neuroscience confirms that trauma physically alters brain structure — particularly the amygdala, hippocampus, and prefrontal cortex. Healing requires approaches that address this neurological reality, not just the emotional narrative.

03

Survivor-led programs consistently outperform clinical-only models in producing durable healing. When those who have lived it design the path through it, something fundamentally different becomes possible.
